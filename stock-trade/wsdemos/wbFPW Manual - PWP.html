What is PatternWorkshop Pro?

PatternWorkshop Pro provides more interactive tools to analyze or to improve PatternWorkshop's simulation results. For example, the software can automatically find the premature conditions to improve its quality and analyze it with your other trading models to see how it affects your whole trading system. It's a key tool kit to develop a high quality trading system, which helps user on how to avoid risk before a signal triggering and how to face the risk after it triggered.

 

In this section we will learn how to read simulation results and how to use related tools. 

  

Simulation Output:

After running a pattern simulation in the input page, the program will automatically (if the simulation takes less than 60 seconds), otherwise one needs to close the stopped output window and re-open it after simulation finished, known from monitor window.  The Simulation Output page looks like the one in Figure 3.1. 

 

On the left side are various statistical numbers based on the signal's daily nets, entry day number of a week volatility and exit methods.  On the right side are the historical net charts of the simulation. The upper one is the net equity chart, the one in the middle is trading volume and the lower one is each trade’s net.  The buttons on the first line of the figure area are used to control where and how to view these charts. The way to use the FT dates keys is the same as other places. Clicking on the Market name button, the program will add the primary market’s daily close line to the upper chart to compare with its daily net, like what is shown in Figure 3-2. When the small button next to the name button on the right is showing 1, the upper chart will always show the daily close line together with the equity line even by clicking the ‘Go’ button.  And the trading list will also be in time order for multi-market simulation instead of market order.

 

On the lower part of the output window, there are various tools for further analysis of the output results.

Any (Decom) operation:

Figure 3.6 is the Any interface.  The two Ok buttons are exactly same for easy access.  In the two long text boxes (U-Any and D-Any), one can enter any statement (That’s why it is called Any operation.).  It can be numbers, any Matlab or FPW functions, or any logic or arithmetic expressions. The statements here are more like what we used in the pattern setup page. We still use i to represent the entry day’s index of the signal.  Any thing used there can be used here.  The number x inside the small text box on the top right is used as scale when outputting the Any distribution.  The spacing will be in x tick-steps of the simulation market, but if a number greater than 10000  is entered for x, it will use x/100-100 as the new scale, which is a market independent number.  The output distribution will overwrite Exit and Volatility sections like the "Time" button does.  Sample Any output is shown in Figure 3.6-1.  The upper sector is for the trades whose (U-Any) - (D-Any) >=0.  The lower sector is for the rest of all trades.

 

For the global variable tradeh or TH, if one wants to use its elements as a variable, one will use ii to represent its row index, i.e. trade order.  For examples:  TH(ii,1)>c(i-3) means if the entry price is higher than its close of three days ago, the logic expression value will be 1, otherwise 0; and TH(ii,8)>=4 returns 1 for all Thursday and Friday’s trades while 0 for the other days.  Entry day’s high h(i) and low l(i) are the high and low at the entry time. Other useful variables are TD, which contains the dates of all trades.  Its format is [mm dd yy] in each row. LABAC - signal triggering order:  [absolute triggering order in a day, current trade's net cumulated position after long (+1) and short (-1) canceling each other in a day, cumulated over night position number] after each trade, it is a 3-column array.

 

When the text box of the far left lower line is in ‘UrSym’, the simulation market itself is used to evaluate the Any cells' expressions.  But when one enters an fsname to it, the program will use that market’s o, h, l, c, v, and other derivative variables to evaluate the expressions.  When a negative scale number is entered to the small text box on the top right, the output will be a full analysis of the trades on the upper distribution sector, it will be like any real simulation output, and called a decomposition model (Decom). Indeed, one can do everything discussed in this chapter again as a simulation output. vf15m is the first 15 minutes volume in 1000 shares.

 

The Any function works in the same way for both Single or Multi-Market modes.  Market name will be shown as F/S-ZZ for Multi-Market mode.

 

Special notes for single market: A lot raw data and pre-processed data, like VCRAF and STATOUT, are available for this case, please refer to variables.

Genetic Optimizers:

We see the Any operation is very useful and powerful, but how does one find proper expressions for it to filter out unwanted trades?   Genetic optimizers are the answer.  They are shown in Figure 3.8. The letter G hyperlink (added recently) will open this manual section when the user need help about genetic optimizers.

 

Figure 3.8 – Genetic Optimizers

 

During a level I(1), level II(12) or level III(123) optimizer run, the neural program uses the last two weeks’ daily opens, highs, lows and closes of the primary market as elements. Then based only on their price levels, it forms all higher or lower possible relationships between each other, and lets them marry each other freely for one, two or three times respectively, with the simple evolving purpose of producing the highest possible equity return or giving out the best model quality.  Their two output tables are the top 20 combined relationships to give the best model quality and the highest final total return respectively.  Model quality is calculated by its yearly final equity times winning percentage and divided by its maximum draw down.  Sample tables are shown in Tables 3.1 and 3.2 for Level I.  For all kinds of optimizer output tables, the notation ‘>’ means equal or higher, ‘<’ for lower, O for open, H for high, L for low, C for close, E for signal’s entry price, A for moving average and M for momentum. The numbers next to the letters indicate the open, or the high, or the low, or the close of  how many days ago.  E0 means the entry price.  Therefore C2>H4, for example, means that the close of two days ago is equal or higher than the high of four days ago. But there is a little difference for A and M. Let us use examples to explain them.  A2>A4 means C1 is equal or higher than its 24 days close simple moving average.  M2>M4 means C1>=C24, i.e. C1 is equal or higher than its close of 24 days ago. On the other hand, we use S to represent SMA (simple moving average) and D for its direction while F for EMA and G for its direction, and the number 1-9 after these for letters (SDFG) to represent 3, 5, 10, 15, 20, 25, 50, 100 or 200 bars average respectively. For examples, S5>S9 means SMA(20)>=SMA(200); D6< 0 means SMA(25) is going down. F4<C1 means EMA(15)<C1. Finally, we use RS to represent RSI(14). For example RS>20 means RSI(14) >= 20.

Besides optimizing the primary simulated market conditions, optimizers can do the same for any secondary market alone or can even further allow their relationships of both the primary and secondary markets to marry each other (please note, it is not their elements, it would take too much computing time to do it, and they are also simply meaningless in most cases). We call this Cross-Market relationship optimizing.  One simply enters an fsname (to replace the UrSym sign, UrSym means to use the simulation market) into the far right text box to introduce a secondary market for the optimizers. 

 

Whenever a secondary market is entered, the level I, II or III optimizer will run over this market instead of the primary one automatically and the output tables are in the same styles as for the Primary Market.  Further, we can also run a level II (level 12x) and level III (level 123x) Cross-Market relationship optimizer by clicking the button 12x or 123x respectively.  Sample output tables for the Cross-Market run are shown in Tables 3.3 and 3.4.  Relationships with ‘>’ or ‘<’ are for the primary market and relationships with ‘G’ or ‘S’ are for the secondary one.  ‘G’ means ‘>=’ and ‘S’ means ‘<’.  Others are the same as for the Primary Market run except one notation ‘R’, which means the relative strength of the primary market to the secondary one.  R2>R4 means the Primary Market’s 24 days relative strength is equal or greater than 1.

 

All Genetic Optimizers work for both single and MM mode simulation outputs, except crossing-market optimizers do not work for MM simulations in the Web version of FPW. During our above discussion, we assumed that the first button in Figure 3.8 is on "New Genetic Run".  But when it is on "Review Results", clicking on "1", "12","123", "12X" or "123X" will bring the previous running results correspondingly, which may be not related to the current studying simulation at all.

For example tick60. For daily and weekly analysis, one can also enter 'ohl' or 'mar' to get only relationships about raw data or processed data. Program set to do daily both raw data and rpocessed data analysis if nothing entered.

Statistics II

Genetic optimizers are tools on studying what happened before signals are triggering, so as to see how to avoid risk.  Here StatisticsII  are tools on studying what happens after signals are triggered, so as to see how to face the risk.  This is about what happens after a signal is triggered.  The purpose is to help the system developer to find the right money managing rules for their models.  The interface is like what is shown in Figure 3.12.  In the first line are the title, command and parameter buttons.  The second line is the summary and subtitle line.  The other lines are the statistical outputs.  The outputs are divided into two groups, the group on the left is for open-to-close statistics, and the group on the right is for the close-to-close statistics.  And in the middle are the statistical topics.  Each group is further divided into two categories: Ups and Downs relative to their group definitions.  From left to right on the first line, the eight forms are: 1st, the time range parameter x for the statistical output; 2nd, C/D button; 3rd, Ok button to get the statistics; 4th, SEO button; 5th, SO Mesh button; 6th, delay parameter y for SEO and SO Mesh; 7th, T/D button for SEO and SO Mesh; and 8th, New run or Review button.  The output data in this page is only related to the daily data and the contents are controlled by the 1st and the 2nd buttons.  The time range parameter x means the x-th day after a signal triggered, which is counted from the signal’s next day for all none-buy-or-sell at the open models, otherwise from the signal day.  It is an integer with a value equal to or greater than 1.  When the C/D button is on ‘D’, the statistics are only for the x-th day relative to its previous day.  And when it is in on ‘C’, the statistics are for x days, it will combine the whole x days’ data into one virtual day and then compare with the signal day or one day before for entering on open or others respectively. Obviously, C and D are the same when x=1. Close(1) means the previous day’s close.  Ok button is used to get the new statistics after changing contents for any one of the first two forms.

 

Figure 3.13  – SEO Output 1

 

SEO outputs graphically the statistical data of maximum draw down, best profit and final equity if the signal is being held for a certain period without any money managing rules applied to.  First, the seventh T/D button decides which database SEO to check, T - intraday or D - daily.  The signal holding period is x days from the first text cell, its definition for a daily run is the same as discussed above. But for a tick data run, x is counted from the signal day and begun from the next bar of the signal entry bar on the entry day.  The delay number in the sixth text cell is used by SEO to postpone its statistical computing periods’ beginning time by y days for daily run, or by y tick bars for tick run from its original defined day or tick bar rules.   For a tick data run, the original simulation itself must be a tick-data based model.  The number y is used just to postpone the SEO calculation but not the signal triggering time itself.  For example, if y=2 and for tick data, SEO runs from the third bar (2 tick bars from the original next bar) to the close of the x day after a signal triggered.  Sample results are shown in Figure 3.13.  SEO results are further divided into two subsets based on whether the best profit or the worst draw down came first during the simulation period, see Figures 3.13-1 and 3.13-2.  From SEO charts, one can find not only some traces of how to set the stop or objective, but also of how to enter a trade in the beginning by observing different delayed SEO results.

Portfolio Manager:

The purpose of this program is to help the system developer choose models correctly when developing a multi-model trading system.  One may find many profitable patterns for the same market, and one must consider their relationships when using them to trade.  The Portfolio Manager’s interface is shown in Figure 3.15. This program works for both future and stock simulations.  It will automatically load all models under the market name one chooses, as long as one saved them under the data filename rule before,  and lists them under the available model list.  One can review any single model by simply clicking on its button.  In order to let a model join all portfolio manager operations, it must be active one, i.e. checked.  The Add All button will add all active models and output as one single model to the simulation output interface.  The Model button will list the model’s (the one showing in the text box next to it in the right.) top 9 (if any) correlated models among all active models. The first column in the correlation report is the model name, the second column tells how many same day signals are triggered as the one we are interested in, the third column is the total signal numbers for each model.

 

Figure 3.15 Portfolio Manager interface

 

Models after Model to reserve label will keep during SORT or combining operation.  The two Clear buttons will clear each of its row’s content or retrieve the most recent cleared lists.  The Sort button will group all active models in any possible combinations which consist of x (the number in the text cell after how many models to combine) models, and outputs its sorting results into 5 categories: best final equity, best model quality, smallest drawdown, shortest flat period and best winning percentage.  It keeps the top 20 combinations for each category and displays the components in the bottom line depending on what is on the view menu at that time.  One can also review the other sorting results by clicking on the Show Me button after choosing proper choices from the two review menus.  Because the sort operation may take a really long time in some cases, the user can monitor it by clicking on the red M button to open a monitor window. The longest button is used to combine all models listed in the bottom line and the models in the Models to reserve, then show the results in the simulation output page, by a limit of y (the number in the text cell immediately after the long button) models or lots (the showing example is 120 models).  When model numbers are larger than y, the program will not take any new position before some old trades are closed.
