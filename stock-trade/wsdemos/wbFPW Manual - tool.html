Detailed Chart
One can get intraday and daily data charts for different time spans. The white bars are for those Friday bars, hourly open bars, the first weekly bars in a month or the first monthly bars in a year.  For intraday bars, only F (the beginning) date is used. One can directly enter a date into the F/T date textboxes using the date format of [mm/dd/yy] or just use the arrows to change dates.  The two inner pairs of arrow buttons on each side of the F or T date move time one bar backward or forward for single click, and 21 bars backward or forward for double click.  The outside pairs of arrow buttons move 50 bars backward or forward for single click, and 1050 bars backward or forward for double click.  The S-Arrow buttons are directly linked with PW simulation output signals to show the earlier or the next signal's intraday data, relative to the current showing signal.  <Z> and <-> buttons are not used in Web version and new features may be assigned to them later. 
 

No matter what the other buttons are set on,  if the last action before clicking the GO button was to change the Future/Stock type button or enter a new market symbol, the output chart will be the whole historical daily chart in our database.  On the other hand, if the last action before clicking the GO button was to change F-T dates, the output chart will be for the specific period, if any.

Leading Indicator

The interface of FPW Leading Indicator (LI) tool is shown in Figure 2.1.  The upper chart displays the Primary Market (PM), which is the one to be analyzed; and the lower one is its leading indicator based on reference markets.  First, let us explain the buttons in the middle.  Clicking on the D (display) push button next to the referencing market (7 of them) will put its data into the lower chart.  The F/S button before each of the 7 referencing markets controls what kind of data that reference market belongs to.  For future markets, one can also use the Def (standing for default) button to load 7 pre-decided reference markets for each PM future market.  They are all based on historical long-term fundamental relationships, but no quantitative rules were decided.  Click on the Run button to calculate the LI for PM based on the 7 reference markets.  White bars are for Fridays.

 

Now let us explain the buttons on the top line.  The second one displays the Primary Market name.  The F/S button before it shows PM is a stock or future.  The D button will display PM’s data in the upper chart.  The number after the label "Mrks" instructs the program how many of the best reference markets to use to calculate the LI, should be <=7.  The number after the label "Cal" shows how many bars to use to calculate the correlative coefficient when compared with the reference markets.  The number after the label "Show" controls how many bars to be displayed into the two charts.  The last two are only for futures.  If the second to last one is Any, it means all other future markets are thought to be the reference markets(about 40 of them in our database for the last Ok button to use).  If it is "Def", it means only some related future markets are used as the reference markets, which could range from a couple to several dozen.  This pre-determined market relationship table is based on the last 15 years of data.  The Ok button acts like Run button for future PM to calculate LI based on pre-decided reference markets.

 

Figure 2.1 – Leading Indicator Interface

 

How is the LI calculated?  1.  Find correlative coefficients with all reference markets.  2. If the best correlated periods of a market lead to the analyzed market and if it takes place after the middle of what the correlative period (number after ‘Cal’), then this reference market becomes a formal candidate.  3. Use the top x (number after ‘Mrks’) best correlated candidates, if any, to calculate the LI.  Then put the top 7 (if any) real used reference markets to the reference market text boxes.  4.  Output LI chart.  A ‘*’ symbol in the chart means that this bar corresponds to the last bar of the analyzed market.  And the Y-label gives the average correlative strength (after S) and how many bars they are leading (after L).

Seasonal Analytic tool: 

Figure 2.3 shows the seasonal analytic tool.  It compares the current year’s up-to-today performance with its corresponding historical statistics graphically.  The listing of years next to the checkbox represents the data available on our database.  The program uses only the data where its checkbox is checked (we call it the active years).  The eight push buttons control how to form the historical yearly cumulative-daily-return.  Checking 1, 3, 5, 10, 20 or 40 years means to use that many active years of data to calculate the historical data, if any. The maximum historical data length is 40 years.  The program will not use any data older than that.

 

Figure 2.3 – Seasonal Output Chart

 

Signal-Noise-Ratio:

Figure 2.4 shows a noise and signal raw data chart.  Signal: abs(c(i)-c(i-1)). Noise: (h(i)-c(i)+o(i)-l(i))/2 for open-close up bars and (h(i)-o(i)+c(i)-l(i))/2 for down bars.  When a Signal Noise Ratio (SNR) is higher than 2, it means one can develop some reliable short-term models otherwise hard to find.

Swing:

Figure 2.5, 2.6 and 2.7 are swing related analysis outputs.  Swing:  market zigzags between pivot highs and lows by definition. It is always in a way of ---high – low – high – low ---.  When a low (high) is lower (higher) than its neighbor’s lows (highs), it is called a pivot low (high).  The level is just the number of its neighbor bars on each side.  Swing analysis is going to find their statistics and give forecasts for the current situation (BigPicture).  To make statistics about its swing life (how many bars a swing lasts) is as important as measuring its swing heights.  Time weight is a factor that is measured by percentage of time contribution when making a forecast.  The swing heights can be measured by absolute change or percentage change.  For BigPicture, one can choose different statistical measurements by clicking the "P-N" button. When the button is on N, statistics are based on the swing’s absolute net change; when the button is on P, statistics are based on percentage change.  Figure 2.7 gives out the swing pivot details graphically.

Zone:

The table below is the output from zone analysis.  There are two kinds of them: 6 zones and nine zones (6 zones plus high, low and close lines).  The six zones are in the way of (0 S2),[S2 S1),[S1 P),[P,R1),[R1,R2) and [R2 infinite), where RS=mean([h(i-1) l(i-1) c(i-1)]); R1=2*RS-l; S1=2*RS-h, R2=RS+h-l and S2=RS+l-h.  They are widely used by floor brokers and short-term traders.  One can also bring yesterday’s open-close up or down as a factor when doing the statistics.  On the title line of the table, YOC means yesterday’s open to close, U for up, D for down and X for no consideration.  LTD-CP: last trading day’s close price, and Z=? is its close zone number.  When we say higher close for zone analysis, we mean from its same day open instead from LTD-CP.  Resistance: if the open is lower and the market never trades into a higher zone, then the higher zone is counted as a resistance.  But for the highest zones (i.e. 6 or 9), it means that a market traded inside the zone but not closed inside it. It is counted as 0 for a zone that is under the open price.  Support: it is just the opposite of resistance.  If the open is higher and the market never trades into a lower zone, then the lower zone is counted as a support.  But for the first zone, it means that a market traded into the zone but closed higher than the zone.  It is counted as 0 for a zone that is above the open price.   Inside a table, number 101 means no historical examples for the case.  Let’s use the first data line to explain how to read it:

 

    C: 83  R: 17  S: 50 and  100 percent chance hit 1183.70 - ZONE 6

 

C (Close): 83 means that 83% chance today would close within this zone, i.e. zone 6 in this example.  R: 17 means that 17% of the historical examples were never higher than this zone during a day.  Because it is the highest zone in this example, this number means 17% events closed below this zone.  S: 50 means 50% of the historical examples (There are 12 events in this sample.) were never traded lower than this zone.  100 percent chance hit 1127.33.  Here it is 100% because it assumed to open at this zone.  The last line in the table makes a forecast for the coming trading day.
