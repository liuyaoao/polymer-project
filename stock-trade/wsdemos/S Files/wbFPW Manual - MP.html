MarketPulse

 

Note: Tick data means intraday data or intraday bars (5 minutes bars in most cases) except specially explained.

MarketPulse is the most basic level of Financial PatternWorkshop (FPW).

 

In today’s market, a successful trader needs an edge. MarketPulse’s proprietary analysis tools let one capture the pulse of the market in real time. Users can easily scan stocks they are interested in by different criteria and parameters, such as exchange, sector, PE ratio, capital size, digital patterns, analog patterns or statistics between any kind of time interval.  Whenever you open your MarketPulse, it will automatically load back to where you left last time.  All scan tools here are for stock markets only.

 

MarketPulse is shown in Figure 4.1.  It is a quick-scan page to simplify and speed up our previous Stock Center performance during the busy intraday trading hours.  MarketPulse is a simple and fast program.  It includes almost everything that our previous version of  Stock Center could do.  MarketPulse is divided into five sections: output displaying area, general filter, quick look, analog scan and digital scan.

 

Figure 4.1 – MarketPulse Interface

 

Output window:

The output section is used to display the output from other sections. In the middle it is just the data of open, high, low, close and so on. No matter what kind of scan one does, the output data is based on the current daily data, except for the last showing column. The blue color rows are up stocks for today, red rows for down stocks. The first number on the title line is the total stock number of the current output. The second column is for the market indicator, N for NYSE, O for Nasdaq or OTC and A for AMEX.  Every symbol name has direct link to what the function menu was on when the scan was done. One can also simply enter a stock symbol into the upper left text cell to get that stock’s information by what the function menu is showing.  The dropdown menu has 12 possible choices.  They are Charts Without Volume, Charts With Volume, Swing Analysis, Seasonal Analysis, Zone Analysis, Analog Analysis, Signal Noise Ratio, Detail Chart, Data Extract, Leading Indicator, Company Profile and Headline News.

 The meaning of scan results of the last showing column depends on where the output is from. FILT is for general filter results. QUIK is for Quick Look scan, the real net change or percentage number for  price or volume during the user defined time period.  Volume is in 100K, price in dollars.  DIGI is Digital Scan. PATN is for Pattern Scan, the number is the coefficient number, 1 means exactly the same as the pattern what one is looking for.  The program only outputs the stocks whose coefficient number is 0.8 or higher.  ZONE is for ZONE Scan (Optional function - ZONESCAN), which is easy to use a real example shown below to explain it.
 

Z-3 M SYMB ZONE LAST ZLOW ZHIGH PRCT SMPLS ZONE
1 O CEPH - 5 57.15 57.66 58.17 86 69 NA
2 O CTAS - 5 46.17 46.66 47.14 77 65 NA
3 O EXPD - 5 40.86 41.14 41.42 80 99 NA


For CEPH, if the next day's open in zone 5 [57.66 58.17), there is 86% chance, it will close lower (indicates by the minus sign before its Zone number) than its open.  During the last 7 years, there are 69 samples of the same situation. For more help with zone analysis, please go to Zone. The zone scan here is based on 6 zones with YOC consideration.

 

General Filter:

The general filter section is on the lower left side.  In the big text area are the filters.  Each one is a logic statement and separated by semicolon ‘;’ sign; its result is either 1 or 0. Users can enter as many conditions as they need, as long as there are less than 19 groups, separated by semicolon.  The program uses AND logic relationships between the groups.  One can use any Matlab or PW function for the logic expression.  But the variables are limited to the following: PE(or pe, PE ratio), CAP(or cap, capital size in billion US dollar), PTB(or ptb, price to book ratio) PTCF(or ptcf, price to cash flow ratio), PTS(or pts, price to sale ratio), BPS(or bps, book value per share), CPS(or cps, cash flow per share), SPS(or sps, sale per share), EPS(or eps, earning per share), TSO(or tso, total outstanding shares in million), SFL(or sfl, floating shares in percentage), SSH(or ssh, short interest in percentage), BETA(or beta, the beta of a stock), NOE(or noe, number of employee), CCODE(or ccode, the company head quarter country code, same as international phone country code, please see below for more details) YD(or yd, yield in percentage points, e.g. 2.5 means 2.5%), SInd, RmSI, KWIN, RmKW and [O H L C V N R](0 1 2 3 4 5 6 7), [AC AR AV HM LM PH PL PO PC](0 1 2 3 4 5 6 7 12 25 75 250). The blue ones can be also in lower case, but can not be mixed up for the blue ones, and will have different meanings. The red ones can be in either case or mixed up.
 

More details about CCODE: Country code can be found at Countrycode.org. For country code '1', i.e. US and Canada, here are the rules: integer 1 means Canada. For US, we have added companies headquarters' first three digits of their zip codes (divided by 1000) to the country code. Therefore, country codes for all USA companies are between 1 and 2. For example, IBM's physical zip code is 10504, so its ccode in our database is 1.105. Then one can use condition (Ccode>=1.9)&(cCode<=1.961) to screen out all California public traded companies in our database. Of course, condition CCODE==972 means to look for all US traded Israel companies. For more US zip code info, one can check at  this zip code map or UnitedStatesZipCodes.org. For some islands nations, '1' added with them. There are five cases in total and their CCODEs are: Marshall Islands - 1692, British Virgin Islands - 1284, Bermuda - 1441, Cayman Islands - 1345 and Bahamas Islands - 1242.

 

For the blue ones: The number 0 means the current bar, and any other number means how many bars ago or during the last how many bars but not including the current bar. One letter plus number ones are for a single bars. Two letters plus numbers are for multi-bars averages or combinations. They are O – open, H – high, L – low, C – close, V – volume, N – net, R – range, AV - average volume, AC - average close, AR - average range, HM - high maximum, LM - Low minimum, PH - percentage of last high in a range,  PL - percentage of last low in a range, PC - percentage of last close in a range, PO - percentage of last open in a range. All values of the percentages are between 0 and 1, 0 means the low and 1 means the high.

For example: C1 - close price of one bar ago; V6 - trading volume of six bars ago; C0 - current bar's close; H0 - current bar's high, N5 - the net of 5 bars ago (i.e. C5-C6); R4 - the range of four bars ago (i.e. H4 - L4). AC25 - the average close of last 25 bars; AV12 - the average trading volume of last 12 bars; AR6 - the average trading range of last 6 bars; HM75 - the most high of last 75 bars; LM25 - the least price among the last 25 bars; PH12 - percentage of current bar's high in the last 12 bars rang [i.e. (H0-LM12)/(HM12-LM12)] ; PO0 - percentage points of current bar's high in the current bar's range. All two letters plus number variables, except when number is 0, are not including the current bar. Therefore, if the percentage number of PH greater than 1, it means that the current bar is making new high in its related range while PL less than 0 for new low in the relate range.

And it's obvious that AC0=C0; AC1=C1; HM0=H0; HM1=H1; and the same for AR, AV, LM and so on when the number is '0' or '1'..
 

Finally, 1) for the blue variables, upper case variables are for Daily bars, while lower case are for intraday bars.  For daily data: V is in 100,000 shares; for intraday data: v is in 1000 shares.  2) PE or pe would be negative for those companies whose trailing twelve months earnings are negative. 3) CAP, PE, YD or others will be zeros, when there are no data available in the database.

 The drop-down selector menu on the top of this group of interactive buttons is used to narrow down which stock group the user is interested in. They are divided into 10 big groups and over 200 subgroups with unique sector index according to their company classifications or related financial indexes.  By choosing the big group title, like Utilities, all its subgroups, like electric, natural gas and water utilities are included. 

 

The other dropdown menu and the four buttons on the right side are used to control the way general filters scan.  The left one in the middle line (the Database button) was used to control which statistics the variables represent, tick or daily statistics.  It is unimportant now after the variable case sensitive setting introduced. Its only function is to quickly change other scan functions' tick or daily buttons at the same time.  The right one on the same line (the Exchange button) controls which exchange or all three (AMX, NYSE and Nasdaq--including over 500 OTC stocks) to scan.  The top left button (Statistics button) opens a intraday market statistics window, which is very useful and is designed for really active short-term traders. For more details, go to MP Statistics.  The Run button runs the general filter scan according to the above rules to output the results to the display area.  The ZoneScan (Optional function - ZONESCAN) button is for scanning the database according to the same above market rules for zone analysis. (Zone itself is a daily data based program, but we can find the stock candidates from general tick database scan, and output the candidates with 85% or stronger confidence candidates to the display area.

 

One can use the links attached to the output top title line or the <SORT> button to sort the results accordingly, if any.

 

Except above parameters one can use in coding ones filters, there are another two pairs very helpful parameters: 'SInd' / 'RmSI' and 'KWIN / 'RmKW'. SInd stands for Sector Index while KWIN for Kew Words in company Name. The other two are the opposite of these two, they stand for ReMove Sector Index and ReMove Kew Words. SInd, RmSI, KWIN, RmKW, PE, CAP, PTB, PTCF, PTS, BPS, CPS, SPS, EPS, TSO, SSH, SFL, BETA, NOE, CCODE and YD are not case sensitive and even can be mixed up when coding them. But all other  parameters for the filter are strickly case-sensitive.

 

SInd rules:
Except sector index chosen from the select form, one can still enter extra index number (or numbers as a vector). One can find each sector's index value from the drop-down selection menu, like the index for all holder stocks is 13.01. Finally, SInd can be only used alone, separated with other logic conditions by ';' sign; and could not mess up with any other parameters, otherwise program will run wrongly or will not run at all. SInd can be only used once in one general filter program, but one can enter more than one index using a number vector, like 'SInd = [3 8.12 1.03 -500];'.

 

The logic relationships between sector numbers:
A) When numbers are all negatives or positives: '|', except case C.
B) When numbers have both negatives and positives: positives do '|', negatives do '&', operated one by one from left to right. except case C.
C) When have -1s involved and no matter how many -1s are issued,
    1) it will be treated only once and will be put in the first, and keep the original order for others.
    2) After new SInd formed, it will always do '&' with the 2nd sector number no matter it is negative or positive.

 

RmSI rules:

Everything is the same with SInd, except:

    1. RmSI does the opposite of SInd, the output will not include the sectors one entered.

    2. '|' (or) relationships between all sector numbers are pre-designed, it does not matter if it is positive or negative.

 

KWIN (Key Words filter) rules:

When to use key words in company names as a filter, one can enter the key words via variable KWIN.  Same as SInd, KWIN can be only used alone, separated with other logic conditions by ';' sign; and could not mess up with any other parameters, otherwise program will simply treat the other parameters just as the key words to search. And KWIN can be only used once in one general filter program. But one can enter up to 4 strings, which are separated by '|' (or) and/or '&' (and). KWIN operates according to the following rules:
    1. strings is not case-sensitive
    2. space(s) in strings is meaningless
    3. logic operates from left to right like (((1)o(2))o(3))o(4), 'o' means either '|' or '&' operation
    4. empty strings will output all stock names index, means no filter or directly passing
    5. will omitted extra sub-strings if more than 4 key words entered, i.e. maximum to enter 3 '|' or '&' operators.

    6. Same like space(s), letters "'" and "=" are meaningless in the strings.
    Example: if entering KWIN='American | fund & income', program will first find Stocks Group 'SG' that contain either 'American' or 'fund', then it will find stocks whose names contain 'income' among stocks 'SG'.

 

RmKW rules:

Everything is the same with KWIN, except:

    1. RmKW does the opposite of KWIN, the output will be the stock, whose company name do NOT include the key words one entered.

    2. '|' (or) relationships between all key words no matter what notation one entered.

 

About daily cutoff time, the output results and others:

The followings are very important, every our user should be very clear about them and always keeps in mind.

1) The daily cutoff time is 4:00pm (All time is in US Eastern Standard Time). Our daily closes are exactly what the last trading prices are at 4:00pm, not the settle prices, therefore the daily nets may be different from your other data servers. Because we believe the last trades at 4:00pm are more meaningful to traders than the settle prices are, although they are almost the same at the most time.

2) After 4:00pm daily update, everything will be treated like that a new trading day has begun. The scan results and the numbers meaning of above variables are all changed. Like V0 will be always the same as v0, and they are trading volumes for the after-hour (4:00pm-8:00pm) or early-hour (7:00am-9:30am) trading. Before next floor trading session, we treat all data during these off-hour trading as one intraday bar. Off-hour data will not be saved to our database and they will be dropped once each these off-hour session closes.

3) In order to help some traders (like ourselves) to review or to study what had happened during the normal floor trading hours at night time,  we set our system back to exactly what  the market is just before our daily update during 4:10pm - 4:30pm and 8:00PM (after after-hour trading closes) to 4:00am next morning (before European users and early birds inn USA begin to work for the new day). Therefore the variable meanings, the output results and so on all looks like they are frozen at 4:00pm just before the daily update for the most whole night.
4) Before running all other scan operations, this general filter will run first to give a group of candidates to speed up their operations.  One should notice that general filter and other operations can be based on different databases.  And General Filter itself can scan both databases at the same time.

Logic expression examples for general filter: 

100*(C0-AC75)./AC75-fpwper('NDX',75,'d')>5; This is for stocks outperforming Nasdaq 100 index by at least 5% during the last 75 trading days. One can change NDX to any index or stock symbol, change '>' to '<' for underperforming, change 'd' to 't' for intraday performance of the reference stock/index when use 100*(C0-ac75)./ac75-fpwper('NDX',75,'t')>5. Here is the online help about fpwper.  Don't forget the '.' before '/', it means vector division.

(PE<=15)&(CAP>=10); Looking for stocks with Price Earning ratio 15 or less (including the negative ones) while their current capital size are 10 billion or bigger.

(C0>ac25); Looking for stocks with their current prices are higher than their average close prices of 25 intraday (cause of lower case) bars ago. Now the intraday bar is in 5 minutes, therefore one is looking for up stocks within the last 2 hours and 5 minutes.

 

Should you have any question about coding your filters, please contact with us. We will help you to write the right codes to find the stocks you want to find.

 

MP Statistics:

This function is designed for really active short-term intraday stock traders, who buy and sell the same stocks again in just a couple of minutes.  This function is a kind of combination among General Filter and Quick Look.  One enters filter conditions in the exactly same way as one does in General Filter.  An example of  a statistics interface is shown below:
 

MarketPulse Statistics
 

Things on the top part are not effected by the filter; it is simply a summary of all the stocks in our database. Our daily closes are exactly what the trade prices are at 4:00pm, not the settle prices, therefore the daily nets may be different from your other data servers.  Except index changes are for the present day,  the time ranges in this part are counted by trading hours, which may expand over different trading days.

The upper most right select menu in the filter part is called a time multiplier. It will multiply the basic time ranges by the time multiplier number.  The basic time ranges are the time ranges when this time multiplier is in 1, they are 1 day and 5, 15, 60 and 120 minutes.  Day's change time is 4:00pm.  Minute ranges are based on floor trading minutes and are calculated by floor trading hours only, they could be over days, e.g. 2 hours means from last trading day's 2:30pm if it is 10:00am now.  All stock links  are to price charts only, but one can reach to anywhere from there.

Finally, click on our logo on the upper right corner to refresh the screen for new statistics, which are continuously updated in the server side; or click on the Today sign in the brackets on the upper left corner to update the top part for today's data only.

 

The quick look section:

This part is aimed to output a quick scan about the most popular questions, such as the most active stocks or the biggest winners or losers in net or in percentage.  In almost all websites or commercial software, all of these statistics one can find are from the daily open or from the yesterday’s close.  It is hard to find the corresponding answers for the last fifteen minutes or half hour on the same questions.  Quick look can give traders a quick answer about these kinds of questions in any time scale.

 

The quick look section consists of four push buttons and five dropdown menus.  The top left one is its exchange menu, and the top right button controls price or volume information for the processing scan.  The left one in the middle line controls data sorting done by net or  percentage.  The right one in the middle line controls the coming scan to look information among the winners, losers or both during the defined time period.  The bottom middle one is its database button. The bottom left three menus work together to provide a way to get number 1-799 by simple mouse clicks.  It can be x days or x intraday bars depending on the database button. The run button makes the scan according to all above conditions plus the general filtering conditions and outputs the top 38 stocks to the displaying area, if any.

The Analog Scan Section (Optional function - Analog Scan):

In this section, one will use the 20 vertical bars to form any shape that one wants a stock daily or intraday close chart to be.  The detailed interface is shown in Figure 4.8.  The up or down arrows are used to change the heights of these bars.  The bars’ heights are from 3-83px. Clicking on the arrows, bar will move up or down by one pixel, double click for 10px. Program will find the stocks with the same close price shape from the related database.  On the top line of this section, there are one menu and three buttons.  The first menu is numbers from 0 to 9.  This number is interpreted as how many new data points will be interpolated between any two original blue bars.  It is a linear interpolation and will keep the shape unchanged.  So the length of the shape will be from 20 to about 200.  The save button to save the current page’s data to a default file.  The next button is its database controller. ‘T’ scans over the intraday database and ‘D’ over the daily database.  The Run button is used to begin a scan. The program will export the best 38 matching stocks to the display area, if any.  The correlative strength will be displayed in the last column on the output window.  The program sets a minimum correlative strength at 0.85.

The Digital Scan Section (Optional function - Digital Scan):

The digital scan interface is shown in Figure 4.9.  The big text area is used to enter codes that define what a user wants to scan.  One can use any Matlab and PW functions here, but the output must use y=1 or y(1)=1 (it is set to 0 by program in the beginning). Codes must follow MATLAB language rules and ends each statement by ';'.  Like many other places, the available variables here are: o - open, h - high, l - low, c - close and v - volume columns, i - the current bar's floating index, ac3, ac6, ac12, ac25, ac75, ac250 (close average for 3, 6, 12, 25, 75 and 250 bars), av3, av6, av12, av25, av75, av250 (trading volume average for 3, 6, 12, 25, 75 and 250 bars) and Xi, Xi6, Xi12, X25, SPXi75, Xi250 (S&P 500 index itself and averages for 6 12 25 75 and 250 bars) and Xi25m - (mtm(ma(SPXi25,2),1)), Xi75m - (mtm(ma(SPXi75,6),2)), Xi250m - (mtm(ma(SPXI250,15),5)). To save space when to code ones pattern, we add i1 at the end of Xi to mean Xi(i-1), i.e. Xii1=Xi(i-1). The same for Xi6, Xi12, Xi25, Xi75, Xi250, Xi25m, Xi75m,, Xi250m, ac3, ac6, ac12, ac25, ac75, ac250, av3, av6, av12, av25, av75 and av250. For example, Xi250mi1=Xi250m(i-1). The meaning of these o, h, l, c, v and all other averages is decided by the T/D button, T is for intraday bars and D is for daily bars. During coding, one could not use any of the following strings as variable names: ii, y1, rToD, cuslenf, mn1, mn2, ftc, NPPV.  They are reserved to the program.  Finally, one must begin the first line by ‘if i>x;’ or ‘if (i>x);’, where x is a positive integer to tell the program that a stock must have at least x bars in its database in order to be a candidate.  In our figure x is 20.  Without this statement, it will not run.  One can save his or her frequently used patterns into files, which can be named to describe the patterns.  The save button will overwrite the old file, if any, without warning.  Delete and load buttons (to popup an interface window for user to choose filename) are used to review or delete a saved file.  All files are saved in the server side.

Program supplies extra variables for you to describe your patterns easily.  They are capital O, H, L, C, V and k - the index of the latest bar. They have the same meaning as o, h, l, c, v and i.  But they are for daily data when running an intraday database digital scan or they are for intraday data when running a daily database digital scan.  Only the latest 20 bars (if any) are available, i.e. one can use index  (k-19) for the earliest past data.  Therefore, it is safe to code the index like max([1 k-x]), where x is a positive integer to access the data of x bars ago. Finally, same as i, if there is data length requirement for k, put it in the beginning like ‘if (i>x)&(k>y);', where x and y are parameters. In addition, several other relatively stable parameters are provided for one to use. They are: 'noe', 'ccode', 'sct' and 'cap'. User has to use them as exactly the same way as they are showing here.
 

Now let's see what stocks one is searching for in the example of Figure 4.9: If current price [c (i)] higher than last 11 bars moving average, if the close of four days ago is higher than the high of seven days ago and if the close of six days ago is higher than the high of nine days ago, then the program will output all of the stocks whose y is 1 to the displaying area.
